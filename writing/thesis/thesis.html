1.2 Abstract

How can sensor data be used to assist the maintenance checks of bridges?

In particular bridge 705?

The lifetime of a bridge is finite. The likelihood of a bridge to fail increases over time until it is no longer considered safe for use. Such an expiry date is an estimate based on expert knowledge, historical data and regular maintenance checks. Sensor data can provide additional information without the delay or cost of a manual maintenance check.

We will research

The risk of underestimating damage to a bridge could be high and thus we need to quantify uncertainty in our model. Notably the risk of a false negative is higher for suggesting a bridge should receive a maintenance check than for predicting a bridge will suffer failure. We will examine the influence on the uncertainty of our damage estimate and the associated risk as we vary parameters of our model. Of particular interest is the increased risk of a false negative ncluding the threshold from low damage requiring a maintenance check to high damage indicating the bridge is close to failure.

Our system, consisting of sensors and associated software, must clearly be cost-effective for it to be used. Therefore we estimate the required impact of such a system on a real-life situation, parameters of such a situation include the amount of sensors, codes/requirements for maintenance checks.

To estimate the damage profile of a bridge…

We will also investigate the ability of predicting damage ahead of time as opposed to real time, … Is a historical damage profile of a bridge sufficient to predict structural damage ahead of time?

Can sensor data reduce maintenance checks to a fixed cost, or at least to a point that it is cost effective?

  • Component level analysis cannot capture entire system level behaviours.
  • Component level maintenance depends on the risk of that component.
  • How can we collect sensor data under normal conditions?
  • Is this information enough to classify abnormal conditions?
  • Can the above be done using NC methods, or time series prediction?
  • Can we classify abnormal conditions early enough that the system is useful?
  • How do the sensor types and positions affect abnormal condition classification?
  • What is the minimum sensor density needed for this system to operate?
  • Can we adapt such a model from one structure (bridge) to another? In particular how much structure specific information is necessary for a useful system?

1.7 Reading & read

1.7.1 2019: Physics-informed neural network: A deep learning framework for solving forward and inverse problems involving nonlinear partial differential equations

The key point in this paper is that when enough data is not available for a deep learning solution, then some law of physics can be included in the error function.

1.7.2 2019: Probabilistic system indentification and optimal sensor placement for a single anchored sheet pile wall

Xuzheng worked on system identification (parameter inference) of a single anchored sheet pile wall from sensor data. A FE model with input parameters of the retaining wall gives a distribution of the observed parameters. The input (or hidden) parameters are then determined with some uncertainty from the observations. Sensor placement is optimised by minimizing the credible region and entropy from the sensor readings.
